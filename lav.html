Lavender Fields of Bhaderwah: The Purple Revolution


Lavender is derived from the Latin word "lavare", which means to wash. It was used in bathing and laundry because of its fragrant properties. Ancient Romans were the first to use lavender for its antiseptic qualities, for deterring insects, and in washing. As a part of the "Aroma Mission" of the Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) and Indian Institute of Integrated Medicine, under the Ministry of Science and Technology, Government of India  in 2010, the lavender flowers were brought to Bhaderwah to uplift local farmers and encourage start-ups. This boost in the cultivation of lavender was coined the "Purple Revolution." It has generated employment as well as brought profit to the local populace. Distillation units have been set up to extract lavender oil. The mild, cold climate of Bhaderwah is ideal for lavender growth. These flowers grow in temperate regions and are drought-resistant. Lavender bears flowers for 15 years and becomes useful in the second year of plantation.

As per the centre’s one district, one product scheme, lavender has been declared as a brand of district Doda of Jammu and Kashmir (UT). The goal of the Purple Revolution was to promote the indigenous aromatic crop-based agricultural economy by shifting from foreign aromatics. The farmers are moving away from conventional cropping to more lucrative lavender farming. Now, the farmers are able to make more profit and enhance their quality of life. Lavender is believed to bring relief in anxiety, depression, stress, dementia, pain, asthma, treating wounds, etc. It is used in cosmetics, for treating blemishes, perfumes, and creams. It is a great natural way for repelling insects and of course, it is better-smelling than other chemical insect repellant options.
